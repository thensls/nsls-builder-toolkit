# Stress-test rubric — 7 dimensions

Used by `bet-idea` step 5. LEANSpark's public guides name seven stress-test
dimensions across a Lean Canvas but don't publish per-dimension rubrics — see
`references/leanspark-stress-test-notes.md`. This file is ours: each
dimension anchored to Strategy Studio's own evidence tags, assumption chain,
and rubric criteria, so a rating always points back at something real in the
engine rather than a vibe.

## The 7 dimensions

**Clarity** — could a stranger repeat the bet back in one sentence, correctly,
after hearing it once? If the one-liner takes qualifying and re-explaining,
clarity is the problem, not the audience.

**Desirability** — evidence of pull, read off the demand-signal ladder (see
`references/diagnostic-questions.md`'s warm-channel section and
`references/customer-interview-playbook-notes.md`'s commitment ladder), never
off enthusiasm. "That's interesting" is not desirability evidence; a
follow-up, an intro, or money is.

**Viability** — a plausible path where revenue exceeds cost, sanity-checked
against `time_to_revenue` from the rubric. A canvas whose revenue box assumes
a sales motion contradicting the buyer's actual purchasing power fails this
even if the numbers on paper look fine.

**Feasibility** — the `build_to_value_risk` question: what has to exist
before the value is even provable? A bet that needs six months of
infrastructure before the first customer can feel the value is higher-risk
here regardless of how desirable the outcome would be.

**Defensibility** — `right_to_win`: relationship, product, or data leverage
that a competitor can't trivially copy. Warm-channel access (existing campus
relationships) counts here directly — it's real leverage, not enthusiasm.

**Mission** — fit with NSLS's identity and core business: does this advance
leadership development, or does it stretch the brand into something members
wouldn't recognize as us? Read against the `exec.core_impact` lens rather
than "could this make money" alone — a bet can be profitable and still be
wrong for NSLS to run.

**Timing** — `thesis.why_now` has to name an actual change in the world (a
new regulation, a shift in how a segment buys, a technology now available) —
not just "we haven't done this yet." If why_now reduces to "it's a good
idea," timing hasn't actually been argued.

## Rating definitions (verbatim, LEANSpark)

- **Red** — "existential risks — things that could kill your business if
  left unaddressed"
- **Yellow** — "known unknowns — you have a hypothesis but no evidence yet"
- **Green** — "validated or low-risk — move on to higher-priority items"

Every rating carries a one-line why — never a bare color — plus a pointer to
a **specific** next validation activity, mapped to our experiment kinds
(`roadshow | pre_sale | concierge | landing_page | pilot | build`) or
interview method, never generic advice like "do more research."

## Sequence matters more than scores

Don't average the seven ratings into a composite and call it done. Instead:

1. Look at every red dimension first.
2. Among the reds, find the most **existential** one — the one where, if
   it's wrong, the rest of the canvas doesn't matter. ("If your Customer
   Segment is wrong, nothing else matters. If your Problem isn't real, your
   Solution is irrelevant.")
3. This is the same domino-effect logic as the causal-chain step's
   `priority` field — the stress test can reuse that ranking rather than
   re-deriving it from scratch.
4. The output is a **prioritized what-to-fix-first list**, ordered by which
   red or yellow to resolve next — not a scoreboard of seven colors.

## This is a prioritizer, not a gate

The stress test never gates a bet's advancement — gates stay evidence-based,
inside the engine (the `research → planned` gate requiring re-scored rubric
criteria at medium+ confidence, and the demand-signal/commitment-ladder
thresholds elsewhere). A bet can carry reds into the gate offer in step 7;
what matters is that the human sees them clearly and the fix-first list
points at real next steps, not that the screen turns green first.

Close every stress test with this, verbatim: **"The goal isn't to turn red
into green on a screen. It's to reduce uncertainty through real-world
evidence."**
