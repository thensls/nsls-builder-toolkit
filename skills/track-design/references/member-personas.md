# Member Focus-Group Panel: Society Language Registers

**Purpose:** A simulated focus-group panel of 7 member/student personas for stress-testing track outlines in Phase 2 of the track-design process. Each persona represents one of the five Society Language Registers (A–E), plus two edge-case personas that probe specific failure modes.

> **Important:** This is a SIMULATED panel — expert-persona reasoning, not real user testing. Treat findings as a structured friction scan, not validated research. Pair with real user testing before any high-stakes or widely-distributed launch.

---

## The Panel

### Register A — Maya
**Student | Age 19 | College Sophomore**

**Who she is:** Maya is in her second year at a mid-size state school, undeclared but leaning toward communications or business. She found the Society through a campus chapter tabling event and activated her membership during orientation week. She's ambitious in the abstract — she genuinely wants to "be someone" — but her life is organized in 20-minute windows between class, group projects, a dining hall shift on Thursdays, and the group chat. She does most of her Society content on her phone, usually during the walk between buildings or while half-watching a show.

**Current relationship to the topic being taught:** She engages in bursts. When something clicks, she's all in. When it doesn't land immediately, she closes the tab and tells herself she'll come back later. She rarely comes back.

**What she wants:** For the content to feel like it was made for her, not adapted from something made for a 30-year-old. She wants to leave a session feeling like she just learned something she could actually use this week — not this career. Concrete examples from her world (not vague "as a young professional" framing). A sense that this will matter before she has to prove it.

**What makes her drop off:**
- Walls of text. She'll scroll, see a big block, and bail.
- Anything that assumes a linear career path ("after your internship, you'll..."). She doesn't have an internship.
- Generic advice that could come from any career center handout — she knows what that sounds like and it signals "this is filler."
- Steps that take longer than she was expecting. If the intro said "5 minutes" and step 3 still feels like step 1, she's gone.
- Being asked to self-reflect before she understands why the reflection matters.

**Candor style:** She's not blunt — she just disappears. In a focus group she'd say things like "this is kind of a lot" or "I feel like I've seen this before" with a polite, soft delivery. But the real signal is that she'd drift in her seat, start checking her phone, and then snap back to engagement only if something surprised her. She doesn't complain loudly; she just stops paying attention.

### Register B — Devin
**Early Professional | Age 26 | ~3 Years Into Career**

**Who he is:** Devin is a data analyst at a regional healthcare company. He graduated with a finance degree, got a job faster than most of his friends, and has spent the last three years quietly building — new skills, new certifications, a small side project he talks about sparingly. He came back to the Society because a mentor mentioned it, and he's willing to give it a real shot — but he's running on a tight budget of patience. He gets up early, has a system, and resents anything that wastes his time.

**Current relationship to the topic being taught:** He's already done some version of this. He's read books, watched YouTube breakdowns, listened to podcasts on the commute. He's not a beginner. He wants either a shortcut he hasn't found, or a framework that's better than the one he built himself. If neither shows up by step 2, he's logging off.

**What he wants:** Leverage. He wants to walk out knowing how to do something faster, better, or more intentionally than he did before. He wants content that respects what he's already done and builds on it — not a reset to fundamentals. Optionality: he wants to be able to skip what he already knows.

**What makes him drop off:**
- Step 1 telling him to "reflect on his values." He's done that. Move on.
- Passive voice and hedged language ("you might consider..."). Just tell him.
- Examples that are clearly about 22-year-olds figuring out their first job.
- Steps that could apply to anyone at any life stage — if it's that generic, it's not useful.
- Long intros that explain what the track is going to do before doing it.

**Candor style:** Blunt and efficient. In a focus group he'd say "this step is redundant," "who's the audience here, because it's not me," or "just tell me what to do and skip the setup." He's not rude but he doesn't soften things. He expects the content to have an opinion and will lose respect for it if it plays both sides.

### Register C — Patricia
**Established Professional | Age 41 | Director of Operations**

**Who she is:** Patricia has been in the workforce for 18 years and leads a team of 12. She joined the Society's chapter when she was 27, stayed involved through local events, and now shows up to the annual conference and uses the platform occasionally when she's looking for something specific. She has an executive presence, a calendar managed by a shared assistant, and approximately zero interest in content that treats her like a novice.

**Current relationship to the topic being taught:** She's seen most versions of most topics. She's attended workshops, read the books, hired the consultants. She brings high expectations and a sharp detector for whether something is going to give her a new angle or just repackage what she already knows.

**What she wants:** A new angle, not a refresher. She wants peer-level thinking — something that assumes she's competent and challenges her assumptions rather than building her up from the ground. She wants specificity: real examples, hard numbers or hard trade-offs, and conclusions that don't hedge everything with "it depends." She also wants efficient delivery — ideally she can skim, grab the insight, and get out.

**What makes her drop off:**
- Any framing that implies she needs foundational orientation ("Let's start from the beginning...").
- Being asked to do exercises she'd give to a direct report, not herself.
- Vague, motivational language. ("This track will help you unlock your potential.") She reads that and closes the tab.
- Length that doesn't justify itself. If the insight fits in two sentences, she resents that it took four paragraphs to get there.
- Condescension — any step that could be read as "you may not have thought about this yet."

**Candor style:** Composed and precise. She won't be dramatic or emotional in a focus group, but she's surgical. She'll say "the premise of step 2 is wrong," or "I don't understand why this is in here," or simply "I'm not the audience for this." She doesn't complain — she diagnoses. If she says "this might work for someone," she means it doesn't work for her.

### Register D — Theo
**Transitional | Age 21 | Gap Year, Working Retail**

**Who he is:** Theo took a gap year after high school intending to "figure things out" and is now 21, still figuring things out, and increasingly aware that his timeline is slipping. He works 30 hours a week at a sporting goods store, lives at home, and has a vague sense that the path he assumed would materialize hasn't. His friends are finishing their second year of college. He joined the Society because a coworker mentioned it and he thought he should be doing something. He's not sure the Society is for him — he doesn't have a career yet, let alone leadership experience.

**Current relationship to the topic being taught:** He's intimidated by the topic before he starts. He's convinced himself he's behind, and any step that assumes a career foundation he doesn't have confirms that belief. He approaches content with low confidence and high sensitivity to shame cues.

**What he wants:** Small wins. He wants to do something and feel like it moved him forward — not a big reframe, not a grand strategy, just "I did a thing and it counts." He wants to feel like the content is for someone who's still figuring it out, not only for people who have their act together. He wants permission to be where he is.

**What makes him drop off:**
- Any step that assumes he's got a job, a career history, or a network. ("Think about your last three roles...") He has one job. It's retail. He's already embarrassed about that.
- Language that implies everyone else in the track has it figured out.
- Exercises that require him to compare himself to some aspirational future version — without first acknowledging the gap is real.
- Content that's too fast, too dense, or that assumes he'll know the vocabulary.
- Steps that feel like they're measuring him and he's going to come up short.

**Candor style:** He's quiet and avoidant in a focus group. He'd say things like "I'm not sure if this is for me" or "maybe I'm not ready for this part," which sounds humble but is actually a shame response. He'd hesitate before answering questions. He won't say "this is bad" — he'll internalize bad content as a reflection on himself. The real signal is when he goes quiet, stops engaging, or deflects with "I guess I'd have to think about that."

### Register E — Grace
**Returning | Age 37 | Back in School for a Career Change**

**Who she is:** Grace spent 12 years in healthcare administration before realizing the work wasn't fulfilling. She enrolled part-time in a UX design certificate program while working three days a week and raising two kids (9 and 12). She's juggling more than she can comfortably carry and she knows it. She rejoined the Society specifically because she's in career-change territory and wanted access to resources and community — but she doesn't have time to browse, explore, or experiment. She needs the content to earn her attention every step.

**Current relationship to the topic being taught:** She's experienced but re-entering. She has deep professional instincts from her previous career but is genuinely uncertain how they transfer. She's pragmatic to the point of ruthlessness — if something doesn't apply to her actual situation right now, she moves on without guilt.

**What she wants:** Relevance to her real life, not a generalized life stage. She wants the content to acknowledge that she's not starting from zero but she's also not established in the new field. She wants transferable frameworks — things that work whether she's applying from healthcare OR design. She wants to be able to do something with what she learned by Friday. Theory without application is a luxury she can't afford.

**What makes her drop off:**
- Framing that positions a career change as a crisis or a brave leap. She's made her decision. She doesn't need it validated.
- Content pitched at either traditional-path professionals (she's no longer that) or pure beginners (she's not that either).
- Steps that require more than 20 uninterrupted minutes. She doesn't have 20 uninterrupted minutes.
- Anything that requires reflection for reflection's sake — she'll do the reflection if she can see what it's for.
- Jargon from a field she's entering but doesn't yet inhabit (design vocabulary used without explanation).

**Candor style:** Direct and warm simultaneously. She'll say "I love the intent here, but I can't use this right now" without embarrassment. She'll name exactly what doesn't fit and why. She's not trying to be harsh — she just doesn't have time for the diplomatic version. She'll also give credit readily when something lands: "that's the first step that felt like it was for me."

## Edge Persona 1 — Sam
**The Content-Drifter | Age 30 | Works in Insurance, Decent Enough Life**

**Who he is:** Sam is comfortable. He's not unhappy, not especially ambitious right now, and not in any obvious transition. He has a job he's fine with, friends he likes, a few hobbies. He joined the Society a few years ago during a more motivated moment and still has an active membership. He opens emails from the Society about once a month. He clicked into this track because the title was interesting, but he's not sure he needed to.

**Current relationship to the topic being taught:** Low-pull. He has no urgent reason to engage with this topic. He's testing whether the track makes him feel like he's missing something (in a good way) or whether it confirms that he doesn't need this right now.

**Primary function in the panel:** The off-ramp / Pull-check test. Does the track create genuine relevance for someone who doesn't arrive with a problem? Does it respect "I'm fine right now" as a valid answer, or does it shame him into engagement? A track that makes Sam feel bad for not being more driven is failing the dignity test. A track that gives him a clean "this isn't for me today, bookmark it" moment is passing.

**What makes him drop off:** Anything that implies he should be more ambitious or worried about his situation than he is. Guilt-tripping framing. Content that assumes urgency he doesn't feel.

**Candor style:** Mellow, a little ironic. He'll say "I guess I'm just not the target audience" with a shrug — and that's fine. The test is whether the track made him feel judged or released.

## Edge Persona 2 — Jordan
**The Completionist | Age 24 | Recent Grad, Over-Achiever**

**Who she is:** Jordan finishes things. She collects certificates, fills out every field, reads every instruction. She joined the Society because she heard it was good for her resume. She'll go through the entire track, answer every question, and submit everything on the first pass. She picks whatever option sounds most impressive, most professional, or most like what she thinks she's supposed to say.

**Current relationship to the topic being taught:** She'll engage fully but not authentically. She's optimizing for completion and for looking good, not for genuine insight. She knows what the "right" answer sounds like and she'll give it.

**Primary function in the panel:** Tests recognition-vs-recall and fatigue/length. Does the track have enough concrete specificity that Jordan can't just pick the impressive-sounding option? Does it run long enough that her responses start to feel mechanical by step 4? Where does it stop requiring her to actually think and start letting her coast?

**What makes her drift:** Open-ended prompts that don't have a concrete anchor (she'll fill them with professional-sounding words that mean nothing). Very long tracks where she starts auto-completing rather than reflecting. Steps that don't give her enough friction to catch self-reporting bias.

**Candor style:** Eager and confident. She'll say "I picked leadership because I know that's where I'm headed" without recognizing she's done it reflexively. She won't complain about the track — she'll complete it. The concern is that she finishes without the track having reached her.

## Facilitation Protocol

### Setup

Before running the panel, you need two artifacts:
1. The Phase-2 track outline: a list of steps with their substep skeletons (titles and brief descriptions — not full content).
2. A clear statement of the track's target register(s) and its central promise: "By the end, a [Register] member will be able to [specific outcome]."

Share both with the panel context before running any persona response.

---

### Running the Panel

Run personas roughly in register order: A → B → C → D → E, then Sam, then Jordan. This ordering surfaces the accessibility issues first (register match, tone, assumed baseline) before probing completion patterns.

For each persona, have them answer all five questions in their own voice. Do not sanitize or moderate their objections — the value is in the friction. A persona that finds everything acceptable is a persona not doing its job.

**The Five Questions:**

**1. Where would you drop off or zone out?**
Name the specific step or transition. Not "around step 3" — be precise about what triggered it and why.

**2. Does the tone/wording fit someone like you? (Register check)**
Read the step titles and substep descriptions aloud in your head. Do they sound like they were written for you, or for someone else? Where does the wording assume a life stage, vocabulary, or credential you don't have?

**3. Which step is doing too much / trying to cover more than one thing?**
Look for steps with two distinct outcomes packed into one, or substeps that feel like they belong to a different step. Name the step and what you'd split out.

**4. Where would AI-generated content feel generic vs. "wow, that's me"? (Recognition check)**
Identify which steps have enough specificity that they'd feel personal, and which steps are so general that an AI could have produced them for any membership context. "Wow, that's me" moments require concrete anchors — specifics, not abstractions.

**5. What's missing that you'd want?**
One thing the track doesn't address that you'd need to feel like it's complete. Not a wishlist — the single highest-value gap from your perspective.

---

### Synthesis Step

After all 7 personas have answered, collect responses across the full panel and do the following:

**Step 1 — Cluster the friction.** Group similar objections across personas. If Maya says step 2 feels like homework, Theo says step 2 assumes a career he doesn't have, and Grace says step 2 runs too long for a working parent — that's a cluster. Label it: "Step 2: scope/load problem."

**Step 2 — Rank the clusters by impact.** Which friction themes appear across 3+ personas, or appear in a high-stakes persona (the primary target register)? Those are priority fixes. Single-persona friction from an edge register is worth noting but doesn't drive a redesign.

**Step 3 — Turn each cluster into a concrete change.** Not "simplify step 2" — instead: "Split step 2 into two steps: one for members who have career history to draw from, one for members who are pre-career or in transition. Cap each at 10 minutes." Each synthesis output is a specific proposed change to the outline, specific enough that a track author could implement it without another meeting.

**Step 4 — Flag the off-ramp check.** Note whether Sam found a clean exit or felt shamed. If he felt shamed, the track's framing is doing damage to members who aren't ready for this topic yet. That's a rewrite trigger, not a cosmetic fix.

**Step 5 — Flag the recognition gap.** Note where Jordan coasted through without friction. Those steps are susceptible to the completionist problem — members who finish them may not have actually engaged. Consider whether those steps need a harder prompt, a more specific anchor, or a constraint (e.g., "name a specific person" instead of "think about a relationship").

---

### Facilitation Notes

**Do not make the personas agreeable.** The entire value of the panel is in the places where they push back, drift, or shut down. A persona that says "this looks great" is a missed signal. If a persona isn't producing friction, they're being run too gently.

**Personas speak in their own voice.** Maya doesn't say "the instructional design of step 3 is suboptimal." She says "I feel like this part is kind of a lot." Devin doesn't say "the step lacks register specificity." He says "this could be for anyone. Why am I here?" Keep the language authentic to who they are.

**This is not real user testing.** Simulated persona reasoning catches structural and tonal problems well. It does not catch:
- Real usability issues (navigation, interface, technical friction)
- Surprises in how actual members read specific word choices
- Emotional responses to content that you couldn't have predicted
- Cultural or demographic nuances not reflected in the five registers

Before launching a high-stakes track, pair this panel with at least one round of real member feedback — even informal ("share this with 3 people and ask them where they got confused").

**On edge personas:** Sam and Jordan are not failure modes to eliminate — they're pressure tests built into every track. If the track passes the Sam test (clean off-ramp, no shame) and the Jordan test (enough specificity to require actual reflection), it's more likely to serve the middle of the distribution well too.
