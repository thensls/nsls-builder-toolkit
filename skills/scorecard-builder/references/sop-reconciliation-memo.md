# SOP Reconciliation Memo — Founder Scorecard SOP vs. NSLS Practice + Research

*For Gary. How the founder's Scorecard SOP holds up against (a) how NSLS actually runs scoring today and (b) the research on scorecards / Topgrading / incentive design. Each item is tagged **DECIDED** (Kevin has made the call), **ADOPT** (build the SOP as-is), or **DISCUSS** (flagged for a founder conversation). Full evidence with citations in `research-grounding.md`.*

## D1 — Core-values scoring: ±15% modifier → **binary Pass / Flag gate. DECIDED.**

**SOP position.** Values sit outside the 100% results weighting and apply as a modifier of up to ±15%: `Final = Results × (1 ± 0.15)`.

**Decision (Kevin, 2026-07-11): NSLS uses a binary Pass / Flag values gate. The ±15% modifier is retired as unworkable.** This matches what production already does — `_shared/references/cpm-constants.md`: *"No ±15% adjustment — values are binary."* So the SOP and the running system were in direct conflict on this point; the conflict is now resolved in favor of binary.

**Why binary, not the modifier.** A ±15% band means a high performer who tramples the values still keeps ≥85% of their results score — likely too small to deter the exact behavior the modifier exists to prevent. Practitioner guidance argues values should carry weight *comparable* to results or short-term results quietly win. A binary gate is cleaner to administer, harder to game, and treats values as a floor (met or flagged) rather than a dial that invites negotiation. Netflix — the company most associated with values-driven performance — uses no values-multiplier formula at all; values and performance are one integrated judgment. Binary is closer to that spirit than a small multiplier.

**What this changes downstream.** The scorecard Doc renders values as a **Pass / Flag** column against each value's MAR — no percentage math, no comp multiplier. Bonus consequences of a flag are an HR calibration decision, not a formula on the card.

## D2 — Values input: one manager's rating moves nothing by itself. **DISCUSS.**

**SOP position.** The Hiring Manager rates each core value (1–4 scale, manager-set MAR).

**What the research says.** A rating is only as trustworthy as its input. Reliable measurement of values-behavior wants **specific observable behaviors** and ideally 360° input, not a single manager's gut call — and the oldest warning in the incentive literature (Kerr) is that hard-to-observe contributions get under-rewarded precisely because they're hard to see.

**Recommendation.** With values now a binary gate (D1), a single flag is high-stakes, so anchor it: ship each value as **observable behaviors** so a Pass/Flag is evidence-based, and allow a **Signal/peer cross-check** to *inform* (not replace) the manager's call. Keep for a founder conversation.

## D3 — "Only KPIs we'd bonus against": right instinct, loosen the payout coupling. **DISCUSS.**

**SOP position.** Put only bonus-grade KPIs on the card — *"choose only KPIs and results we would be willing to commission or bonus against."*

**What the research says.** The instinct (don't clutter the card with things you wouldn't stake comp on) is correct. The risk is the tightness of the metric-to-pay coupling — the best-documented failure mode in the field: Goodhart's Law ("when a measure becomes a target it ceases to be a good measure"), Kerr's *Folly of Rewarding A While Hoping for B*, MBO sandbagging, and real blowups (Wells Fargo, VW). Goodhart is a *tendency, not an iron law* — avoidable with good metric design. And a top-tier study (Zureich 2023) finds holding people accountable for *uncontrollable* factors can actually improve decisions — so "only what they control" is a strong default, not an absolute.

**Recommendation.** Keep the SOP instinct — only bonus-worthy outcomes on the card — but **loosen the payout mechanism**: tie only a portion to a balanced subset, pair every quantity metric with a quality counterweight, keep a human-judgment overlay rather than a pure formula, and satisfice targets. Retire "commission" as the framing word ("bonus against" carries less gaming baggage).

## Guardrails applied during drafting (alignments with the SOP, not divergences)

- **Outcomes, not tasks.** Strongest evidence in the field: specific hard goals beat "do your best" (Locke & Latham, d = .42–.82).
- **Controllability check.** Goal specificity only reduces performance variance insofar as performance is controllable — flag accountabilities outside the person's control (with D3's Zureich counterpoint for when broad is acceptable).
- **Learning vs. outcome goals for novel work.** On new/complex work a hard number causes tunnel vision; a learning goal works better — steer such areas to a capability accountability.
- **The vital few.** SOP's ≥10% weight floor caps the card near 10; research pushes tighter (EOS caps at 5–15 numbers; sprawl is the #1 Balanced Scorecard failure). Target **3–6**.
