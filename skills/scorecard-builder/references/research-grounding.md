# Scorecard & Topgrading — Research Grounding

*Source: deep-research workflow (108 agents, 26 sources, 3-vote adversarial verification). 70/75 claims confirmed, 5 refuted. This doc = the evidence base to cite inside the scorecard skill.*

---

## 1. Topgrading (Bradford D. Smart)

**Canonical prescription.** Topgrading replaces vague job descriptions with a **job scorecard** (mission + outcomes/accountabilities + competencies), also used as the basis for coaching conversations. Sorts talent into **A/B/C players**. Uses a **chronological interview** (up to 4 hrs for managers) covering every job's successes, failures, mistakes, key decisions, so strength/weakness patterns emerge; the **TORC** (Threat of Reference Check) technique deters exaggerators. Topgrading holds A-player managers should be strong on **~50 competencies** vs. the 6–8 typical interviews assess.

**Confirmed:** scorecard-replaces-job-description; A/B/C framework; TORC; chronological method; the 50-competency *doctrine* (properly scoped as "Topgrading asserts…").

**REFUTED / caveats (do NOT cite as fact):**
- ❌ "**~4,000 data points vs <200**" — vendor marketing; it's Topgrading's own arithmetic (12 Q × 20 jobs × 15 competencies), not an empirical measurement. No independent corroboration.
- ❌ "**Chronic B/C players should not be hired**" — overreach. Canonical A-player = *top 10% of talent available for the role at the target compensation*; B/C can sometimes be developed.
- ⚠️ Wikipedia lists **6–8 competency categories** (Leadership, Organization, Company Fit, Peer Relations, Resourcefulness), while Smart's books use the ~50-competency taxonomy. Both true at different granularity.

## 2. EOS / Traction (Gino Wickman)

**Canonical prescription.** The **EOS weekly Scorecard** = 5–15 measurables reviewed weekly at the Level 10 meeting — an *operational* tool. **Measurables** are activity-driven **leading** indicators (vs. KPIs as **lagging** indicators). **Every role gets ≥1 measurable; one owner per number** ("if two people own a number, no one does"). The **Accountability Chart** defines seats first; **GWC** (Get it / Want it / Capacity) tests right-seat fit; **core values** (3–7) define "right people."

**Key distinction confirmed:** the EOS *weekly operational* scorecard ≠ a *role/performance* scorecard — though EOS's own glossary doesn't draw that line explicitly. **NSLS's scorecard is the role/performance kind, not the weekly-numbers kind.**

## 3. Outcomes vs. Activities — the hard evidence (Locke & Latham goal-setting theory)

**Canonical, peer-reviewed (Locke & Latham 2002/2006, the theory's originators):**
- Specific, **hard** goals beat easy or "do your best" goals — positive **linear** relationship between difficulty and performance. Meta-analytic effect sizes **d = .42–.82**. "Do your best" fails because it has no external referent.
- **Boundary condition — controllability:** goal specificity reduces performance variation **only insofar as performance is fully controllable** by the person.
- **Moderators:** feedback (to track progress), commitment (self-efficacy + importance), task complexity. A scorecard must *supply progress feedback and secure commitment*, not just set a number.
- **Failure mode:** on **new/complex tasks**, a specific outcome goal causes **"tunnel vision"** and interferes with acquiring needed skills — a *learning* goal works better there.
- **Goal conflict:** personal goals *incompatible* with the group's goal **hurt** group performance. Bonusing a manager on their unit over the whole org is a named example of destructive goal conflict.

→ Strong empirical backing for the SOP's "outcomes not tasks, with a clear metric." With two caveats: controllability, and learning-heavy/novel work.

## 4. Tying metrics to pay — best practice AND failure modes

**The foundational warning (Kerr, "On the Folly of Rewarding A While Hoping for B", primary):** reward systems routinely pay off for the behavior the rewarder is trying to *discourage*. Fascination with **simple, objective, quantifiable** metrics causes **goal displacement** everywhere except highly predictable areas. Hard-to-measure work (team-building, mentoring, creativity) goes unrewarded *because* it's hard to observe. First remedy is **diagnostic** — audit what's actually being rewarded.

**Goodhart's / Campbell's Law:** "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." People optimize the metric in ways that decouple it from reality. Real cases: cobra/rat bounties, VW emissions, Wells Fargo accounts, Uber.
- ⚠️ **REFUTED as stated:** degradation is *not* deterministic/universal — it's a **tendency, avoidable by metric design**. (The refutation matters: good design mitigates it.)

**MBO paradox / sandbagging:** tying pay to goal attainment makes people **set easier goals** (punished for missing ambitious ones).

**Controllability — with a twist:**
- Classic principle: hold people accountable only for what they control.
- **BUT Zureich 2023 (Journal of Accounting Research, top-tier peer-reviewed):** paying on *uncontrollable* factors can *improve* decision-making by making people attend to and learn from those factors — stronger when the factor is informative and the person is goal-focused. Explains why broad, uncontrollable metrics are common and work. → Controllability is a strong default, **not an absolute rule**.

**Recommended safeguards (converging across sources):**
1. **Decouple / loosen** the metric-to-pay linkage; tie only a *portion* of incentive to a *balanced subset*.
2. **Multiple counterbalancing metrics** (pair quantity with quality — tickets closed + CSAT/ticket).
3. **Pair leading drivers with lagging outcomes.**
4. **Combine quantitative with human judgment** (soft metrics, peer review, case studies).
5. **Rotate/evolve metrics**; keep weights somewhat uncertain; **satisfice** rather than maximize.
6. **Measure outcomes over outputs;** build causal metric trees that expose gaming.

## 5. Strategy → KPI cascade (and the "scorecard" name collision)

**CRITICAL disambiguation:** the **Kaplan-Norton Balanced Scorecard** is a *strategy-execution* tool (4 perspectives: Financial, Customer, Internal Process, Learning & Growth) — **NOT** the Topgrading/role hiring-performance scorecard. Same word, different tool. NSLS's is the **role/performance** kind.

**BSC cascade mechanism (Kaplan-Norton, primary/HBS):** build a **strategy map of objectives FIRST**, select measures after; objectives linked in **cause-and-effect** from people→process→customer→financial (2–3 intermediate stages = line-of-sight/driver logic). Cascades enterprise → business-unit → team/individual.
- **Nonprofit-critical:** for nonprofits, **financial is NOT the top objective — mission/social-impact goes at the top** of the strategy map; mission = the org's accountability to society. (Directly relevant to NSLS.)
- **Failure modes:** metric sprawl (80+ KPIs); lagging-only scorecards (can't course-correct); **Analog Devices** — every quality metric improved while the stock fell ~70% (improving metrics with no causal link to value).

**OKRs (Doerr):** emphasize measurable **outcomes**, not prescribed activities. **Rigid top-down cascading is an anti-pattern** — it assumes strategy is perfect, is slow/expensive to adapt, sidelines support functions. Preferred: **alignment** — teams set their own metrics respecting parent *intent*; top-level OKRs = shared North Star. (Maps directly onto NSLS "LOP alignment" language.)

**Hoshin Kanri:** X-Matrix (decompose) → Bowling Chart (track); longer 3–5 yr horizon; catchball involves lower levels in setting objectives.

## 6. Values as a modifier

**Practitioner canon — the performance-values 9-box:** rate **"what" (results)** and **"how" (values/behaviors)** on **two independent axes**. Ideal = high/high; high-results/low-values = coaching intervention. Broad agreement that values belong **separate from** raw results.

**BUT — tension with NSLS's ±15% multiplier design:**
- Expert guidance (Emergenetics CEO) recommends weighting values **comparably** to performance — "a similar value on *what* is accomplished and the *spirit* in which it's accomplished." A **±15% modifier is much smaller than co-equal weighting** → risk that "short-term results take precedence," which is the exact culture erosion the SOP is trying to prevent.
- **Netflix** (often cited as the values exemplar) actually does the *opposite* of a formula: **no bonuses/KPI pay at all** (market-based comp), and **integrates** values + performance in one judgment (keeper test) rather than a values multiplier. So Netflix is *not* precedent for a values multiplier.
- **Operationalization requirement:** each value must become **specific, observable behaviors**, and reliable measurement typically needs **360° feedback** from managers + peers — not a single manager's gut rating. Manager training is the named implementation risk.

## Verdicts on the three NSLS design choices

**(a) Accountabilities weighted to 100%, ≥10% floor → Results Score.**
✅ **Well-founded.** Matches goal-setting evidence (specific/weighted beats vague) and the "vital few" principle (EOS 5–15, BSC anti-sprawl). The ≥10% floor enforces focus. *Guardrails to add:* keep the total count small (≈3–6); ensure metrics are within the person's **control** (or consciously accept Zureich-style uncontrollables); pair any single quantity metric with a **countervailing quality** check; confirm each accountability has **one owner**.

**(b) Core Values as ±15% multiplier: Final = Results × (1 ± 0.15).**
⚠️ **Defensible in spirit, but two evidence-based concerns.** (1) **Magnitude:** ±15% is *far smaller* than the "co-equal weighting" experts recommend to actually protect culture — a high performer who tramples values loses at most 15%, which may not deter. Consider a wider band, a **hard gate** (values below MAR caps or zeroes the bonus), or the 9-box as a cross-check. (2) **Measurement:** a defensible modifier needs values expressed as **observable behaviors** + ideally **360° input**, not one manager's rating — else the multiplier is arbitrary. The *separation* of values from results is correct; the *thinness and single-rater basis* are the risks.

**(c) "Only KPIs we'd be willing to commission/bonus against."**
⚠️ **Right instinct, dangerous literalism.** The instinct (only put comp-grade outcomes on the card) enforces quality. But the evidence says **the tighter you bind a metric to pay, the more it gets gamed** (Goodhart, Kerr, MBO paradox, sandbagging). Safer framing: choose bonus-*worthy* outcomes, then **loosen the payout formula** — portion-based, balanced subset, human-judgment overlay, satisficing targets. (The gaming risk is org-agnostic and applies fully to NSLS as a for-profit; the earlier nonprofit-ethics angle does not apply.)

**Overall:** NSLS's model is a coherent Topgrading-scorecard + weighted-results + values-modifier hybrid. The structure is sound and evidence-aligned. The sharpest risks are all in the **comp linkage**: gaming from tight metric-to-pay coupling, a values modifier that may be too thin and too single-rater to do its job, and nonprofit-specific "commission" exposure.
